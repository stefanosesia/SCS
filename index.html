Autonomous returning rockets collision avoidance

This webapp is aimed at simulating an additional safety system to the spaceX Autonomous returning module. This is based on the data I gathered from the successful launches of the Falcon 9 rocket and the security procedures put in place for the occasion.

Demo

Falcon 9 - stage 1 module return

This demo will strongly focus on the Falcon 9 rocket from Space X. This rocket is able to autonomouly adjust its trajectory and return back to earth, landing on a specified platform (either on mainland or on a drone boat). Space x conducted 51 missions to date 49 of which were successful 23 of which had a reported successful self landing.
Truly a feat of engineering, thi rocket is able to acheive this thanks to its 9 "Merlin" thrusters and a sophisticated positioning system.
After delivering its payload on orbit, the rocket naturally flips itself with the help of a small burst from one of its engines(Boost back burn), when that is acheived and the correct allignment is confirmed, the thruster switches off and the rocket starts descending. Still at supersonc speeds the Falcon 9 uses 3 of its merlin engines for the so called Reentry burn. This is the first space craft to use boost from thrusters at supersonic speeds to slow down instead of deploying a parachute and NASA is studying gathered data aiming to soon use this technology to safely bring men to mars. finally, approaching the landing platform, the positioning system carefully calculates the landing and uses one merling thruster and lateral flaps to land the rocket in its original vertical position.

Current security procedures

As traditional positioning systems based on ground are not precise enough for this manouvres, especially at these speeds, the Falcon 9 carries it's own GPS and sensors, elaborating them on its on-board computer. As the system for now solely relies on sesors already packed in the rocket, NASA and SpaceX are usually also deploying aircrafts with infrared cameras to monitorthe landing phase.
Civilians aricrafts are kept safe by the establishment of a vast no-fly zone tht should keep commercial flights safe.
With spacex aiming at making such launces more frequent and even planning to land on ground (the falcon managed such a feat 11 times already), the no-fly zone will have to be restricted to a small window and be as narrow as securely possible. This makes sense if taking air traffic into consideration.

This simulation will revolve around alternative emergency measures should the no-flight zone not be respected or the rocket malfunction getting out of the safe area.

Navigate possible actions

Thrusters Failure
Risk description

  • The thrusters and aerodynamic flaps are damaged.
  • Unforeseen wind conditions have put the rocket out of its correct allignment.

This could have happened due to a human error on the preparation phase or due to impacts or over-heating during the launch.
Action

If the rocket is still inside of the safety perimeter and can crash without causing substantial damage (such as landing in the ocean), then all systems are switched off and the fall is monitored. Otherwise the supersonic parachute is deployed to try and slow down the fall.

Risk description

  • The thrusters are out of fuel.
  • Unforeseen wind conditions have put the rocket out of its correct allignment.

This could have happened due to a leak in the fuel tanks or a miscalculation of the required fuel.
Action

If the rocket is still inside of the safety perimeter and can crash without causing substantial damage (such as landing in the ocean), then all systems are switched off and the fall is monitored. Otherwise the flaps are used to try and reposition the rocket on its original trajectory.

Navigation Failure
Risk description

  • The on-board and backup computers are irresponsive or faulty.
  • The GPS module stopped transmitting or is getting a wrong position.

This could have been due to overheating during the lauch phase or a short circuit in the electronics.
Action

Three ground GPS and radar systems should be deployed around the safety area (on floating platforms or drones if the return is scheduled on water) to provide an emergency tracking system. An emergency communiction module should be put in place to trigger the basic systems.

Should thrusters and flaps still be responsive, they should be triggered to steer the rocket to crash inside of the safety perimeter.

Should steering not be a viable option the supersonic parachute should be deployed to break the fall.

Should all other option fail, the rocket would still be tracked and areas could eventually be evacuated
